Chapter One

It starts with
a city.

Mumbai has a food culture unlike anywhere on earth. It's chaotic, democratic, unapologetic.

A Vada Pav costs less than a pound and tastes better than a £30 restaurant dish. A Karak Chai at 7am changes your entire day. Nobody queues politely — you push, you point, you eat standing up, and you feel alive.

The streets of Mumbai aren't just where people eat — they're where people gather, argue, laugh and belong. Every corner has an adda — a spot. A tapri, a stall, a cart that knows your order before you speak.

That city, that energy, that food — it gets into your blood. And when you're 5,000 miles away in South London, you miss it more than you could have imagined.

Chapter Two

When the
world stopped,
she cooked.

"I just wanted to feed people. To give them something warm when everything felt cold."

When COVID-19 arrived in 2020, it took away almost everything — routine, connection, comfort. For Blossom, it gave her something unexpected: time. Time to cook the way she'd always wanted to.

What started as home-cooked meals for neighbours and friends quickly became something bigger. Word spread. More people knocked. More orders came in. The community was hungry — not just for food, but for something that felt like care.

Every dish she made carried a piece of Mumbai — the butter chicken her family grew up with, the Schezwan noodles she'd eaten on late nights in the city, the Vada Pav that reminded her of mornings at the station. Real food, made with real love.

In her own words

"I cook because I love it. Because food is how I say I care about you. Every dish I make carries a memory, a feeling, a little piece of the city I grew up loving."

Blossom didn't set out to run a business. She set out to cook food that made people feel something — the way Mumbai street food had always made her feel.

Mumbai Adda is personal. It's her adda, shared with Sutton. It's the place she hopes makes you close your eyes on the first bite and think — yes. That's exactly what I needed.

"When someone tells me my Butter Chicken reminded them of home, or that they drove across Sutton just for the Saturday Biryani — that's everything. That's why I do this."

— Blossom, Founder, Mumbai Adda Sutton
The name

What is an
adda?

In Mumbai — and across the Indian subcontinent — an adda is more than a place. It's a feeling. Your corner. Your spot. The chai tapri where the uncle knows your order before you speak. The bhelpuri stall where the aunty adds extra sev because she likes your face.

अड्डा

An adda is where people gather, eat, argue, laugh and belong. It doesn't need to be fancy. It just needs to feel like yours.

Blossom didn't just open a takeaway. She opened Sutton's adda — a little piece of Mumbai dropped into South London, where the food is real, the welcome is warm, and every order is made with love.
